I had the privilege of leading a cross-party delegation of MSPs to California last week to learn more about how assisted dying laws are working throughout the state.

The California End of Life Option Act came into force in 2016 and gives dying citizens the choice of an assisted death should they meet strict eligibility criteria.

Like the bill I will introduce to the Scottish parliament in coming weeks, the Californian law is open to terminally ill, mentally competent adults only. Other similarities include self-administration of medicine and the agreement of two separate doctors.

Over the course of four days the delegation met families of people who had used the law; doctors and specialists involved in supporting patients, and the senators who wrote the act
