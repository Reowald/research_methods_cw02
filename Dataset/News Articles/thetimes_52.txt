Conservationists in the Central American country are racing to buy up a fast-vanishing ribbon of forest that is vital to the survival of wildlife

A few miles inland from Belize’s Caribbean coast lies a thin stretch of land known as the Maya Forest Corridor. After a decade of agricultural expansion in the surrounding area, it is the last thread connecting Belize’s northern rainforest with the southern Maya Mountains and it has become the focus of a dispute over the nation’s future.

Conservation groups such as Re:wild, championed by the actor Leonardo DiCaprio, argue that the corridor is vital for wildlife. Without it, they say, jaguars, pumas and tapirs would be stranded in the mountains. Unable to travel in search of mates, they would steadily lose genetic diversity and resistance to disease.
