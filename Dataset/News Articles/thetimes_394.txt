Horatio Bottomley was the most entertaining rogue in British history. Born in Bethnal Green in 1860, he lost both parents by the age of five, grew up in an orphanage and worked as a court reporter before setting up his own publishing business and launching the Financial Times. He owned racehorses, consorted with chorus girls, promoted gold-mining shares and embezzled millions — and ordinary people loved him for it. Once, at a shareholders’ meeting, he was asked what had happened to £700,000 missing from the accounts. “I haven’t the faintest idea,” Bottomley said, and sat down to applause.
